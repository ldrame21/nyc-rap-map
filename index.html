A cartography of New York as conveyed through rappers’ lyrics

As done by Moretti for the 19th century neighborhoods of London, a cartography of a city as it is depicted through litterature can teach us a lot about the feelings and connotations associated to it. In Morretti’s case, it allows him to notice a strong difference between the real London, crumbling under poverty and crime, and the one described in contemporary novels. Moretti’s study allowed him to map feelings associated to given places at given times. By applying a methodology similar to his’ to a 21th century topic of interest, with new tools and approaches, we were hoping to visually represent … . In our case the city to cartography therefore became New York, as for the literature and novels, they became rap lyrics.

Background

“There are some who dismiss hip-hop as the dead letter of brazen stereotype-mongering among the severely undereducated and their gaggle of learned and over-interpreting defenders. Other critics claim that the deficits of hip-hop are amplified because they blare beyond the borders of ugly art to inspire youth to even uglier behavior. And others protest that, stripped of politics, history, and racial conscience, hip-hop is little more than sonic pathology that blasts away all the achievement of the civil rights struggle.”

Born between Harlem and the Bronx around the 70s from a mix of different cultures and art forms, hip hop is surprisingly rarely considered as a subject of scholarly studies. Indeed, since its apparition rap has often been criticized for corrupting the younger generations by twisting their ideals. By considering the inherent negativity of the genre as the translation of a harsh environment and understanding it in close relation to a context, we can understand rap as a mirror of the society. This is reflected in the speed at which it evolves and reacts to events of the actuality. It thus makes sense to use its lyrics as a reservoir from which to draw information specific to decades and boroughs.

For his study mentionned above, Moretti proceeded to a large survey of sentiments associated with precise locations within London. All of his data was drawn from 15’000 lines of various types of books, written between 1’700 and 1’900. These were then associated to a score ranging from -1 to 1, with -1 being very negative to 1 being very positive. Basing our methodology on Moretti’s, we exchanged the books for a dataset constructed using lyrics from about a hundred of New York City’s hip hop artists. In the latter, artificial paragraphs consisting of three consecutive “lines” of their lyrics were created, in order to increase their contextualization and reduce false interpretations. We then proceeded to keep only the paragraphs which mentionned a place in New York City, resulting in a dataset containing a total of 3’200 paragraph.

Topic Extraction

The chart presented above is a topic model created using TFidf. It was made to explore the different topics addressed in New Yorker rap lyrics in relation with different factors. The lyrics lines we started from were separated by lines, we therefore had to find a way to artificially create the different corpuses with specially distinct topics required to perform topic modelling using TFidf.

Our first step was to separate the lines per neighborhood, in hopes that clusters describing New York boroughs would appear. The results were unfortunately not concluding as we couldn’t see nor understand any logic within the clusters. A probable explanation for this is that the topics of larger interest were recurrent in most of the neighborhoods, making them disappear through the TFidf method.

The last and final step consisted of creating two main corpuses, each separated in five sub-corpuses. The first of the main corpuses contained lines related to New York City in general as well as the most famous locations in the city, such as Broadway or SoHo. The second main corpus aggregated lyrics precisely describing NYC neighborhoods, respectively the Bronx, Harlem, the Queens, Brooklyn, Brownsville, Long Island and Staten Island. This classification resulted from the assumption that descriptions made by rappers regarding the City, as well as the context in which these were used, were different from the ones of more precise neighborhoods. As for the sub-corpuses, they were separated as follows: very positive, positive, neutral, negative and very negative. Two categories were added to the previous calculations to add nuance the statements and make more topics appear.

Analysis

The analysis of the texts allows us to differentiate several facets of rap. When the rappers mention a locality in New York, the range of topics is wide, and covers the most positive to the most negative of what we know of the rapper's background. To claim that the rapper only talks about money and drugs in his lyrics is therefore proven wrong by this analysis. A more detailed analysis of these texts allows us to know whether the rapper's imagination resembles the real conditions of the communities in question.

There are four topics of society that we have profiled in the topics:

  • The daily routine

    In which various topics of the rapper's life are clustered, from family, to neighborhood.

  • Music, the main income of the rapper

    Just like an average citizen participating in the working world as we know it, the rapper concentrates the majority of his time on producing music. The work of a rapper has many outlets, and is a profitable field for him and his entourage (management, branding, production, logistics, ...)

  • The love of the community, the neighborhood

    The MC is the first witness of the living condition of his community. He is also considered as a model of success, and can sometimes take the cut of benefactor of his neighborhood. He represents both a geographic space and often fringed communities in that seeks pride in rap competition.

  • The danger of the lifestyle/day routine

    As a reference, we can look at the important crime rate (murder, rape, assault, etc..) recorded in the Bronx and compare it to the average rate of the city of New York. The rate of Bronx is approximately 3 times higher. The rappers experiencing it on a daily basis become field journalists, often witnesses of shocking events, and sometimes even the protagonists of this violence. Their songs and lyrics can often be understood as reports of what they live and see and become an important source of information. (ref)
Now that we have succeeded in identifying a range of topics discussed in rap, we can analyze geographically the variations of emotions applied to New York City neighborhoods.

Methodology

Joining both the topic approach and the time approach, we propose the following heat map to get a better sense of how artists depicted the city throughout the last 4 decades. This way one can browse throughout the decades to see the evolution of the shared sentiment concerning New York’s boroughs.

Mean sentimental compound value for different categories

In these box plots, we can see that the imaginary communicated throughout the lyrics varies according to the localities mentioned. These graphs suggest that, according to the imaginary narrated by the artists, certain NY neighborhoods are talked about in a more positive way than others.

A stereotype that is often associated to rap lyrics is that they are always rather negative and inciting violence. They become positive when the rapper conveys pride in being from the neighborhood he or she “praises”.

"Free flowing and stomping! Never tip-toeing Overthrowing the comp Big up Bronx!"

In the quote stated above as an example, KRS-One glorifies the neighborhood he is from. This feeling of belonging expressed in the lyrics is a possible claim of unification in opposition to another possibly larger groupment, such as the rest of the city or the police for example. We can notice this innate opposition particularly in rap through its writing style. Other positive topics include the desire to grow as a family or the desire to grow financially.

“You got a gat, you better find it And use that shit, think fast and get reminded Of robberies in Manhattan, you know what happened Sixty G's worth of gun clappin'”

In contrast, we often find hints of hustle, illegal activities in negative sentences. The example stated above is one of the sentences pulling down Manhattan's score in Mobb Deep’s track Drop a Gem on ’Em.

Harlem Case study

“I grew up in the slums of greed Known for Drawin guns with speed or Sellin' tons a weed cuz I got sons to feed” Hell up in Harlem, Big L

One of the observations we can make on the heat map is that we notice a clear evolution from decade to decade in the way rappers sing about the neighborhood of Harlem. In fact, the borough got a very negative score in the 80s, but improves its positivity score through the decades. There are various possible explanations to this evolution.

BEFORE BRUNCH, THERE WERE RIOTS
The improvement of life in Harlem could be one of them. Over the sixties and seventies, Harlem experienced a series of severe crises. With many of its inhabitants fleeing poverty and riots resulting from the political climate and inequalities, in hopes to find a safer place with better infrastructure. The poorest and least educated population with no means nor will to leave however remained, living in a climate of perpetual tension. Statistics and testimonies from the 70s reflect the deterioration of living conditions in Harlem. But since 1980, efforts have been made to stop this crisis. Federal public funds have been distributed to revitalize northern Manhattan as with the "New York Empowerment Zone" in 1996. The objective being gentrification in the neighborhood to make Harlem attractive. Looking only at numbers, it was a successful bet because the crime rate has since then highly diminished in Harlem. One could however argue that a part of the authenticity and culture of the borough has also been lost, this is something we would have liked to notice in our results, we however were unable to draw such conclusions from our results.

THE HORRORCORE RAP STYLE
Another possible explanation of such a negative score in the 80s could be the ongoing rap lyrical movement at the time: the horrorcore rap style. Quoted above, Big L was one of the most influential artists of Harlem. He was born in 1974 in a Harlem deep in a social crisis. His lyrics are raw, and he is often credited to have been a part of the creation of horrorcore, a violent and dark style of rap in which people use graphic phrasing to talk about murder and violence. This wave disappeared after the 80s.
