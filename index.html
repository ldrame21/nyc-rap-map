A cartography of New York as conveyed through rappers’ lyrics

As done by Moretti for the 19th century neighborhoods of London, a cartography of a city as it is depicted through litterature can teach us a lot about the feelings and connotations associated to it. In Morretti’s case, it allows him to notice a strong difference between the real London, crumbling under poverty and crime, and the one described in contemporary novels. Moretti’s study allowed him to map feelings associated to given places at given times. By applying a methodology similar to his’ to a 21th century topic of interest, with new tools and approaches, we were hoping to visually represent … . In our case the city to cartography therefore became New York, as for the literature and novels, they became rap lyrics.

Background

“There are some who dismiss hip-hop as the dead letter of brazen stereotype-mongering among the severely undereducated and their gaggle of learned and over-interpreting defenders. Other critics claim that the deficits of hip-hop are amplified because they blare beyond the borders of ugly art to inspire youth to even uglier behavior. And others protest that, stripped of politics, history, and racial conscience, hip-hop is little more than sonic pathology that blasts away all the achievement of the civil rights struggle.”

Born between Harlem and the Bronx around the 70s from a mix of different cultures and art forms, hip hop is surprisingly rarely considered as a subject of scholarly studies. Indeed, since its apparition rap has often been criticized for corrupting the younger generations by twisting their ideals. By considering the inherent negativity of the genre as the translation of a harsh environment and understanding it in close relation to a context, we can understand rap as a mirror of the society. This is reflected in the speed at which it evolves and reacts to events of the actuality. It thus makes sense to use its lyrics as a reservoir from which to draw information specific to decades and boroughs.

For his study mentionned above, Moretti proceeded to a large survey of sentiments associated with precise locations within London. All of his data was drawn from 15’000 lines of various types of books, written between 1’700 and 1’900. These were then associated to a score ranging from -1 to 1, with -1 being very negative to 1 being very positive. Basing our methodology on Moretti’s, we exchanged the books for a dataset constructed using lyrics from about a hundred of New York City’s hip hop artists. In the latter, artificial paragraphs consisting of three consecutive “lines” of their lyrics were created, in order to increase their contextualization and reduce false interpretations. We then proceeded to keep only the paragraphs which mentionned a place in New York City, resulting in a dataset containing a total of 3’200 paragraph.

Topic Extraction

The chart presented above is a topic model created using TFidf. It was made to explore the different topics addressed in New Yorker rap lyrics in relation with different factors. The lyrics lines we started from were separated by lines, we therefore had to find a way to artificially create the different corpuses with specially distinct topics required to perform topic modelling using TFidf.

Our first step was to separate the lines per neighborhood, in hopes that clusters describing New York boroughs would appear. The results were unfortunately not concluding as we couldn’t see nor understand any logic within the clusters. A probable explanation for this is that the topics of larger interest were recurrent in most of the neighborhoods, making them disappear through the TFidf method.

The last and final step consisted of creating two main corpuses, each separated in five sub-corpuses. The first of the main corpuses contained lines related to New York City in general as well as the most famous locations in the city, such as Broadway or SoHo. The second main corpus aggregated lyrics precisely describing NYC neighborhoods, respectively the Bronx, Harlem, the Queens, Brooklyn, Brownsville, Long Island and Staten Island. This classification resulted from the assumption that descriptions made by rappers regarding the City, as well as the context in which these were used, were different from the ones of more precise neighborhoods. As for the sub-corpuses, they were separated as follows: very positive, positive, neutral, negative and very negative. Two categories were added to the previous calculations to add nuance the statements and make more topics appear.

Analysis

The analysis of the texts allows us to differentiate several facets of rap. When the rappers mention a locality in New York, the range of topics is wide, and covers the most positive to the most negative of what we know of the rapper's background. To claim that the rapper only talks about money and drugs in his lyrics is therefore proven wrong by this analysis. A more detailed analysis of these texts allows us to know whether the rapper's imagination resembles the real conditions of the communities in question.

    What words appear the most in the quotes?

    Showed below are 3 wordclouds generated in the subdataset where the subject revolves around women.

    Firstly, 2015, when MeToo did not exist yet. The main words we see are related to family, and "sexual assault" and "domestic violence" appear in small.

    Then, 2017, the year the movement was created. We see new terms appearing (e.g. "sexual harrassment", "sexual misconnduct"...). It is expected for such words and bigrams to blow up since we are at the peak at the movement and any other result would have been unexpected.

    Finally, 2019, when the movement is supposed to be past its peak. The main words we see go back family-related ones, but now keywords related to MeToo are more existent than in 2015. It is a great result to see, because even though the main subject around women is back to where it was, we can see some influence that the movement had on the content of the quotes after 2017.

    What topics appear the most in the quotes?

    With the help of NLP libraries, we extracted the main topics present in Quotebank, when the subject revolves around women. For this, tokenization and lemmatisation have been applied and we took into account both monograms and bigrams only. Some topics related to MeToo appear (e.g. Harvey Wienstein, sexual abuse, marriage equality...). This is indeed promising, and shows a certain influence of the movement. But it is not a statistically strong claim to say that MeToo had such a big impact that it became a full topic in of itself. The closest our clustering has come to is in cluster 8, where for &lambda = 0.85, we can infer a topic centered around men-women relations, where men can sometimes go too far.

    To Conclude...

    Going back to our main question: Did MeToo have any impact on the place of women in media?

    The studies conducted above can lay a strong argument for the possibility that MeToo did have an influence. However, this has to be taken with a grain of salt. The rise in women speakers, the shift in sentiment, the slight change in word usage might be a result of what transpired in October 2017, but it could also be that these results, along with the explosion of the MeToo movement, are both caused by something outside the scope of our study and Quotebank.

    Quotebank dataset

    The data explored in this story comes from the Quotebank dataset. This dataset represents a collection of 178 million quotations from various English speaking news articles. Every quote has then been sanitized and analyzed by a machine learning model named Quobert, based on the NLP model BERT [2] , developed by Google. Quotebank contains probabilities of potential speakers for each quote that was captured from 2008 to 2020. It is publicly available here.

    Data Exclusion Rules

    Data included in this story may differ slightly from other published reports due to certain data decisions. For the purposes of these analyses, the Quotebank dataset has been manipulated as follows:

    1. The whole speaker-attributed dataset from 2008 to 2020 has size around 32.8 GB. This would be too much to handle to create a datastory at our scale. Also, the MeToo movement does not happen until the latter part of the 2010's, which means it would be a waste of memory and time to use all data since 2008.

    2. Since Quotebank assigns to a single quote different speakers with different degrees of certainty, the speaker with the highest probability is kept for the datastory, and the other speakers are discarded.

    3. Quotebank sometimes attributes certain quotes to an "Unknown" speaker. The quote is discarded if "Unknown" is the only possibility. If there are multiple choices, the most certain known speaker is kept and the rest are discared.

    4. A large filtering of the quotes has been done. Since the story revolves around MeToo and the place of women in media, 2 subdatasets have been created with keywords-based filtering:

  • A subdataset representing quotes where women are mentioned or talked about.
  • Another much smaller subdataset where only MeToo-related keywords were fed in order to obtain much more representative quotes towards the movement.
